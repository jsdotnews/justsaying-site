How to read us

A simple card structure

  • Claim: the exact line that’s circulating, quoted precisely.
  • Record: what was said or done, with time and a link to the source.
  • Why it matters: two sentences that explain the stakes without a lecture.
Standards

Receipts first

Primary links go first: video, transcript, docket, or the speaker’s own post. If context was cut, we note it. Labels come last.

Short & shareable Source-linked Neutral tone

Claim: “Charlie Kirk advocated stoning gay people to death.”

Record: In June 2024, Kirk cited Leviticus 20:13 on his show, described it as “God’s perfect law … when it comes to sexual matters,” and added “just sayin’.” That is a quotation of scripture, not an explicit call to enforce stoning under U.S. law. After his death in September 2025, Stephen King posted that Kirk “advocated stoning gays,” then shifted toward saying the clip showed selective use of scripture.

Why it matters: Quoting a text and urging modern enforcement are not the same. The difference was lost in the reaction cycle, and the claim spread quickly without that context.

Sources: Daily Beast, PBS NewsHour, LGBTQ Nation, White House proclamation

Image/Meme: viral screenshot about A.

Why it matters: A clean source line prevents a screenshot from standing in for the full event.
