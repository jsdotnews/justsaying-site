119th Congress · H.R.22

SAVE Act — Safeguard American Voter Eligibility Act

Sponsor: Rep. Chip Roy (R-TX) Introduced: Jan 3, 2025 Senate Floor — Vote This Week

This bill would require every American to show physical documents — like a passport or birth certificate — to register to vote in federal elections. The House has passed it twice. The Senate could vote as soon as this week.

01

What Would Change?

The Main Rule

Right now, you sign a form saying you're a U.S. citizen when you register to vote — no documents required. Under this bill, you'd also have to show physical documents proving it, in person, before you're registered.

What's "Federal Elections"?

This covers elections for President, U.S. Senate, and U.S. House. Most major national elections. State and local elections are a separate matter and not covered by this bill.

No Documents? There's a Backup

If you can't show the required documents, you can sign a sworn statement saying you're a citizen — under penalty of perjury — and submit other evidence. A local election official then decides if it's enough.

Penalties

Election officials who register someone without checking citizenship documents face criminal charges. And under the bill, any citizen can personally sue an election official who registers someone without proper proof.

How the Legislation Got Here

The original SAVE Act (H.R. 22) was introduced in January 2025 and passed the House in April 2025. It stalled in the Senate. Republicans then introduced an expanded version — the SAVE America Act — which passed the House in February 2026. That version now sits before the Senate, with a vote expected this week.

The SAVE America Act goes further than H.R. 22: it also adds voter ID requirements to cast a ballot on Election Day, not just at registration. The core document requirement for registration is the same in both versions.

What current law already requires: Under the National Voter Registration Act of 1993, all voter registration forms already require applicants to state, under penalty of perjury, that they are U.S. citizens. Non-citizens who vote in federal elections are already breaking federal law. The SAVE Act adds a document-verification step on top of that existing requirement.

What Documents Would Count?

The bill specifies which documents prove citizenship. These come directly from the bill text. You'd need at least one of the following:

01
A REAL ID-compliant license or ID that specifically says you are a U.S. citizen Note: Most standard driver's licenses are REAL ID-compliant but do not show citizenship. Only enhanced driver's licenses (available in some states) indicate citizenship.
02
A valid U.S. passport or passport card
03
A U.S. military ID card plus a military service record showing you were born in the United States
04
Any government-issued photo ID that shows your U.S. birthplace For example: an ID that lists "Place of Birth: United States"
05
A photo ID plus a certified birth certificate (meeting specific requirements listed in the bill) The birth certificate must include full name, date of birth, place of birth, parent names, an authorized signature, filing date, and the issuing government's seal.
06
A photo ID plus a Naturalization Certificate or Certificate of Citizenship
07
An American Indian Card issued by DHS with a 'KIC' classification
Source: All document types listed above come directly from the text of H.R. 22 as passed by the House. Read the full bill text on Congress.gov →

What People Are Saying

These are arguments made by supporters and critics. Just Saying News does not add its own position.

What supporters say

The Case For

✓ Non-citizens voting in federal elections is already illegal, but the current system relies on the honor system — a sworn statement with no document check. Supporters say this bill closes that gap.
✓ Passports and similar IDs are already required for many federal activities. Supporters argue registering to vote is no less important.
✓ The bill includes a backup process for people without standard documents, so no citizen who is truly eligible would be permanently blocked.
✓ President Trump has called this a top priority. The White House argues it is necessary to restore confidence in elections.
What critics say

The Case Against

✗ The Brennan Center for Justice estimates more than 21 million U.S. citizens do not have the required documents readily available — meaning they'd have to obtain them to register.
✗ The bill would effectively end mail-in voter registration, online registration, and registration through voter drives — since those methods can't collect physical documents in person.
✗ The National Association of Counties estimates the changes would cost states $510 million or more per election cycle — without any federal funding provided in the bill to cover it.
✗ Critics argue that credible studies consistently show non-citizen voting in federal elections is exceedingly rare, and the existing legal penalties are a strong deterrent.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between the SAVE Act and the SAVE America Act?

The original SAVE Act (H.R. 22) focuses on voter registration — you'd need to show citizenship documents to sign up to vote. The SAVE America Act is an expanded version that passed in early 2026. It keeps the registration document requirement and adds voter ID requirements to actually cast a ballot on Election Day. The Senate is currently considering the SAVE America Act version.

Do I need to show documents to vote right now?

No. Under current federal law, you sign a form swearing you're a citizen when you register — but you don't have to show documents. Some states have their own ID requirements at the polls. This bill would add a document requirement at the registration step for federal elections, which is a new layer that doesn't exist today at the federal level.

Would people who are already registered be affected?

The document requirement in H.R. 22 applies to new registration applications. People already registered under existing rules would not automatically need to re-register. However, the SAVE America Act's additional provisions on voter ID at the polls would affect everyone who votes in federal elections if it becomes law — exact details depend on the final bill text.

What if someone doesn't have a passport or birth certificate?

The bill includes a backup process: you can sign a sworn statement under penalty of perjury and submit other evidence of citizenship. A state or local election official then decides if you've proven your citizenship well enough to be registered. Some advocacy groups argue this backup process is not a reliable safety net for all voters.

Does non-citizen voting in federal elections actually happen?

Federal law already makes it illegal for non-citizens to vote in federal elections, with criminal penalties. Multiple independent studies have found that instances of non-citizens voting in federal elections are rare. Supporters of the bill say documenting eligibility upfront is a reasonable safeguard regardless of current rates.

What would "nuking the filibuster" mean here?

Normally, 60 Senate votes are needed to end debate and bring a bill to a final vote. Since Republicans control 53 seats, they'd need 7 Democratic votes — which they don't appear to have. Some Republican senators and the White House have publicly discussed changing Senate rules to require only a simple majority (51 votes) to pass this bill. As of this writing, whether they will do so is not resolved.

How do I stay updated?

You can track H.R. 22 directly on Congress.gov, which will show any Senate action. We'll also update this page when the Senate votes.
