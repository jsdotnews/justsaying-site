What it does

The bill, in plain English

No spin. Here's what the actual text says.

📋

The main change

Right now, when you register to vote, you sign a form saying you're a U.S. citizen. No documents required. Under this bill, you'd also have to show physical proof—like a passport or birth certificate—in person, before you can be registered.

🗳️

Which elections?

Federal elections only—President, U.S. Senate, and U.S. House. Not state or local elections. But in most states, you register for all elections at once, so this would affect most voters.

📄

No documents? There's a backup.

If you can't show the required documents, you can sign a sworn statement—under penalty of perjury—and submit other evidence instead. A local election official then decides if it's enough to register you.

⚖️

Penalties

Election officials who register someone without checking citizenship documents can face criminal charges. Any citizen can also personally sue an official who registers someone without proper proof.

📜

Two versions—what's the difference?

The original SAVE Act (H.R. 22) only covered voter registration: show documents to sign up. The SAVE America Act—which passed the House in February 2026—keeps that requirement and also adds a photo ID rule to cast a ballot on Election Day. The Senate is debating the SAVE America Act version this week.

ℹ️ What the law already says: Under a 1993 federal law, registration forms already require applicants to swear, under penalty of perjury, that they are U.S. citizens. The SAVE Act adds a document-check on top of that existing sworn statement. Non-citizen voting in federal elections is already a federal crime.
The specifics

What documents would count?

Taken directly from the bill text. You'd need at least one of these to register. Read the source on Congress.gov

01
A REAL ID-compliant license or state ID that specifically shows U.S. citizenship Most standard driver's licenses are REAL ID-compliant but do NOT indicate citizenship. Enhanced driver's licenses—only offered in some states—do.
02
A valid U.S. passport or passport card
03
U.S. military ID plus a military service record showing U.S. birthplace
04
Any government-issued photo ID that shows your U.S. birthplace Example: an ID that states "Place of Birth: United States"
05
A photo ID plus a certified birth certificate meeting the bill's specific requirements Must include full name, birthdate, birthplace, both parents' names, authorized signature, filing date, and official government seal.
06
A photo ID plus a Naturalization Certificate or Certificate of Citizenship
07
An American Indian Card issued by the Department of Homeland Security with a 'KIC' classification

What supporters say

The case for it

✓
Most Americans support this. Multiple polls find 80%+ favor voter ID—including majorities of Democrats. A Harvard/Harris poll found 71% support this specific bill, including 69% of independents and about half of rank-and-file Democrats.
Harvard CAPS/Harris Poll ↗    Gallup: 84% favor voter ID ↗
✓
Voter ID laws haven't reduced turnout in practice. After Georgia passed a strict voter ID law in 2021, the state had record turnout in both 2022 and 2024. A large 2021 study by the National Bureau of Economic Research reviewed a decade of data and found voter ID laws "have no negative effect on registration or turnout, overall or for any group defined by race, gender, age, or party affiliation."
National Bureau of Economic Research ↗
✓
A bipartisan commission co-chaired by former President Jimmy Carter recommended voter ID in 2005, writing: "The right to vote is a vital component of U.S. citizenship, and all states should use their best efforts to obtain proof of citizenship before registering voters."
Carter-Baker Commission Report ↗
✓
You already need an ID for many things: boarding a plane, entering federal buildings, opening a bank account. Supporters say registering to vote is no less important.
H.R.22, Congress.gov ↗
✓
A backup process exists for people without standard documents: sign a sworn statement and submit other evidence. Supporters say no eligible citizen would be permanently blocked.
Bill text, Section 2 ↗
What critics say

The case against it

✗
Tens of millions of citizens don't have these documents handy. A 2023 survey found 21.3 million voting-age citizens don't have proof of citizenship "readily available"—meaning documents might be in a safety deposit box or a relative's home. About 3.8 million don't have them at all. Note: This survey was conducted by the Brennan Center, which explicitly opposes voter ID laws.
Brennan Center (liberal advocacy org) ↗
✗
The problem this bill is trying to solve appears to be very rare. Utah reviewed all 2+ million of its registered voters and found one confirmed noncitizen registration and zero noncitizen votes. Louisiana found 79 possible noncitizen votes over 40 years out of an estimated 74 million total ballots cast.
Bipartisan Policy Center ↗
✗
Registering by mail, online, or through a voter drive would effectively end—because those methods can't collect physical documents in person. Military members serving abroad would also have a harder time registering.
CNN ↗
✗
States and counties estimate this would cost over $510 million per election cycle to implement—with no federal funding in the bill to cover it.
National Association of Counties ↗
✗
The bill takes effect the moment it's signed. Critics say this would disrupt the 2026 midterms, which are already underway—primaries have started and absentee ballots are already in the mail.
CNN ↗

FAQ

Plain-language answers

Who made this page and what's their position?

Just Saying News is an independent site with no political affiliation. We don't take ads, we don't endorse candidates or bills, and we don't lobby. Our goal is to put the actual facts in one place so you can decide what you think. Every source on this page links to the original document. If you spot an error, we want to know.

Is the Senate voting right now?

Not exactly. The Senate is in floor debate this week—senators are speaking, making arguments, and going on record. That's not the same as a final vote. The final vote hasn't been scheduled as of March 16, 2026. The debate could last days or even weeks. This is still the right time to call your senator, because they're actively working on this bill right now.

What's the filibuster and why does it matter here?

In the Senate, most bills need 60 votes to even move to a final vote. This is called the filibuster. Republicans control 53 seats. They need 7 Democrats to cross over—and Democrats have said they won't. Unless Republicans change the rules (which several of them have said they won't do either), this bill is expected to fall short. It doesn't mean the fight is over—it can come back.

Is this bill bipartisan?

In Congress—barely. The most recent House vote passed 218–213 with only one Democrat (Rep. Henry Cuellar of Texas) voting yes. In opinion polls, it's a different picture: surveys from Gallup, Pew, and Harvard/Harris all find broad majority support—including large shares of Democrats and independents—for voter ID requirements.

Do I need documents to vote right now?

No. Under current federal law, you sign a form when you register saying you're a citizen—but you don't have to show documents. Some states have their own photo ID rules at the polls. This bill would add a new national document requirement for registration that doesn't exist today.

Would people who are already registered be affected?

The registration document requirement applies to new applications—people already registered wouldn't need to re-register right away under the original H.R. 22 language. However, the newer SAVE America Act also requires voter ID at the polls and voter roll verification, which would affect currently registered voters too.

If it passed and was signed, when does it kick in?

Immediately. The bill says it takes effect the moment the President signs it. Critics say this makes the 2026 midterms nearly impossible to administer because states would have to change their systems overnight, and voting has already started in some states.

How do I track what happens next?

The best place is Congress.gov—they update in real time and you can sign up for email alerts when the status changes. We'll also update this page.
